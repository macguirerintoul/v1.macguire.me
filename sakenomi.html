Sakenomi

What

A product design, UI design + programming project on creating a “smart device” to augment the atmosphere of a home

When

April 2016

Who

Josh Stuible
Ga Yan Yip
Rina Shimohashi

Where

IAT 267 at SFU

Why

IAT 267 is an introductory Arduino course offered at SFU. The final project involves researching, using sensors, prototyping a form, and programming the Arduino and a corresponding interface using Processing. There were 5 design streams to choose from, and our team jumped at the opportunity to create a smart device for the home. Considering topics such as child security, home security, and hygienic convenience, we decided to pursue somewhat of an experience design project – the Sakenomi.

Our design precedents were Flume’s Infinity Prism, dollar store mood rings, and lava lamps. We were fascinated by the emotional effects that light and colour can have on people, as well as the illusion that one’s mood and the visual phenomenon are somehow correlated.

Our final product, the Sakenomi, is a Japanese-style mood lamp. In discussion, the team agreed on the cleanliness and simplicity of Japanese design in architecture and products, and decided to emulate it. The physical form of our lamp was inspired by a Japanese pagoda, with the cladding of the lamp made to resemble a Shoji screen.

It gathers data from the attached sensors, and adjusts its colour and brightness to match the mood of its environment. We used a microphone to measure the noise in the room to control brightness, passive infrared sensors to measure activity to control colour temperature, and an RFID sensor to detect different users’ RFID tags to switch to their preferences.

Our research included studies on colour temperature on rodent brains, and the effects of brightness on energy levels and mood. According to our research, we created an RGB colour map that dictated the colour on the axes of motion and sound.

Our end result was a minimal lamp that was able to accurately interpret the atmosphere of the room, making it a beautiful addition to any room for warm relaxing light or an energetic party booster.

Future iterations

Due to time restraints and difficulties wiring the sensors, we could not add a beats-per-minute detection feature which would enable the lamp to pulse to the beat of music being played.
